# Competitor Visual Benchmark Audit - I-0232

This audit treats the named benchmark books as market expectations, not as a claim about exact figure counts or page designs. The question is simpler: what must *Next Token* do visually so it feels more useful, more physical, and more memorable than the shelf around it?

## Verdict

The current visual program can exceed major AI/business books on mechanism clarity and claim discipline, but it is not yet ready to beat the shelf as a reading experience. The gaps are physical texture, human/object memory, map/place usefulness, rights readiness, caption elegance, and page rhythm.

The strongest advantage is the book's authored visual language: systems diagrams, source-surface cards, blocker maps, token-economics boards, and stable figure IDs. The danger is that this strength becomes visible apparatus. A reader should feel guided, not audited.

## Benchmark Pressure

Chip-history and factory-history books set the standard for physical constraint. *Next Token* already has the intellectual machinery: accelerators, supply chains, AI factories, power, cooling, and token economics. To exceed that standard, the final layout needs material interruptions: a rights-cleared physical surface, a source document, a facility/geography cue, or prose breathing room exactly where schematic density peaks.

Energy and infrastructure histories set the standard for maps and bottlenecks. The current book has good flows, but not enough place. A map-like exhibit is valuable only if it explains interconnection, siting, export chokepoints, energy routing, or supply constraint. Decorative infrastructure imagery would weaken the project.

AI histories and biographies set the standard for memory. People remember objects, pages, labs, launches, interfaces, and moments of theater. The selected ledger has many screenshot and product-surface slots, but they remain production debt until capture, hash, rights/private-use notes, and claim-safe captions are closed.

## Current Score

The scorecard in `data/competitor_visual_scorecard_i0232.tsv` gives the book high marks for industrial mechanism clarity and source-surface credibility. It gives low marks for rights readiness, final render proof, physical/human texture, and map/place usefulness.

That pattern is healthy at this stage. It means the book has a distinctive visual mind, but the next visual passes should not add more diagrams by reflex. They should select, prune, humanize, and prove.

## Practical Standard

Every final visual should pass at least one of four tests:

1. It orients the reader in time, place, system, or institution.
2. It proves a claim with evidence the reader can inspect.
3. It humanizes or materializes an otherwise abstract machine.
4. It slows the page at a moment where prose alone would blur.

Any figure that only demonstrates research diligence should be cut, moved to apparatus, or converted into a source note.

## Immediate Implications

The benchmark audit turns the existing page-flow defects into market risks:

- Chapters 2-5 are not merely sparse; they risk feeling like a technical preface before the story has warmed up.
- Chapters 15-16 are not merely dense; they risk looking like a corporate/infrastructure dashboard.
- Screenshot slots are not merely unfinished; they are memory assets or rights liabilities, depending on whether provenance closes.
- Source-card candidates are not merely backup; they may be the safest way to beat the shelf on trust.

The next passes should use this audit as a gate, especially for reader-addiction review, prose-around-figures editing, final rights triage, and visual readiness recomputation.
