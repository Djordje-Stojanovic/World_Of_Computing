# Datacenter Physical-Site Evidence Board - Pass I-0175

This pass treats site imagery as evidence with a narrow job. A satellite image can show visible geography. It cannot show what model was trained, how many GPUs are inside, how much power is flowing, what fuel was burned at a given hour, whether a permit was satisfied, or whether a campus is economically justified.

## Strongest Visual Direction

The safest final exhibit is not a spectacular commercial satellite crop. It is a house-style evidence grid that teaches the reader how to read physical-site imagery:

- Location: what source anchors this site?
- Footprint: what can the image visibly show?
- Power/cooling: what equipment is visible, if any?
- Time: what changed between dated images?
- Blocked claims: what remains unproven?

That grammar could then support later licensed imagery for xAI Colossus, Stargate Abilene, or Microsoft Fairwater without letting the picture overclaim.

## Source And Rights Posture

For free or lower-risk imagery, USGS/Landsat and NASA/USGS sources are the first stop, but their resolution may be too coarse for data-center campus details. OpenStreetMap is useful for locator maps if ODbL attribution is handled correctly; it is not a source of satellite imagery. Google Earth, Bing, Maxar, Apple Maps, and other commercial imagery should be treated as license-needed, not as quick screenshots.

Named sites need separate source anchors:

- xAI Colossus: use xAI source surfaces and MLGW utility context, but do not write turbine, GPU-count, power, or emissions claims without permit/regulatory evidence.
- Stargate Abilene: use OpenAI/Crusoe project sources for the announced campus story, with all capacity and chip figures source-attributed and not inferred from imagery.
- Microsoft Fairwater: use Microsoft's official site story for the cloud-capacity narrative, not as neutral proof of "world's most powerful" or operational status.

## Chapter Placement

Chapter 16 should get the main physical-site treatment because it owns power, cooling, interconnection, and useful capacity. Chapters 8, 9, 10, and 11 can reference named infrastructure only where it explains company strategy or compute constraints.

Generic rights-safe images may beat named sites for final art. A public-domain substation, cooling yard, or transmission photo can make the physical stakes vivid while avoiding the evidentiary traps of a named AI campus.

## Next Acquisition Gate

Before any final site image enters the exhibit pool, the row needs: date-stamped source, license or public-domain proof, geolocation confidence, source hash or screenshot path, caption, story purpose, and blocked-claim note. If any of those is missing, the image remains an acquisition candidate, not a publishable exhibit.
